    Section 15.2 Markov chains

    We will introduce the concept of a Markov chain by example.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 15.2.1.

    Suppose there are two chains of grocery stores, \(W\) and \(C\text{,}\) and that each month
    • 90\% of people that shop at \(W\) continue to shop at \(W\) in the next month,
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • 10\% of people that shop at \(W\) will switch to \(C\) in the next month,
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • 70\% of people that shop at \(C\) continue to shop at \(C\) in the next month, and
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • 30\% of people that shop at \(C\) will switch to \(W\) in the next month.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    This is represented graphically in FigureÂ 15.2.2.
    ðŸ”—
    Suppose that initially 100 people shop at \(W\) and \(80\) people shop at \(C\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Question: How many people shop at \(W\) and \(C\) in the following month?
    ðŸ”—
    For short, we will say that a person is in state \(W\) if they shop at \(W\) that month, and in state \(C\) if they shop at \(C\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{align*} \amp \# \text{ of people in state next month} \\ \amp \qquad\qquad = 0.9(\text{# of customers}) + 0.3(\text{ of customers}) \\ \amp \qquad\qquad = 0.9(100) + 0.3(80)\\ \amp \qquad\qquad = 90 + 24 \\ \amp \qquad\qquad = 114, \end{align*}
    and
    \begin{align*} \amp \#\text{ of people in state next month} \\ \amp \qquad\qquad = 0.1(\text{ of customers}) + 0.7(\text{ of customers}) \\ \amp \qquad\qquad = 10 + 56 \\ \amp \qquad\qquad = 66\text{,} \end{align*}
    which answers the question. Note that
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 0.9 \amp 0.3 \\ 0.1 \amp 0.7 \end{bmatrix} \colvec{100}{80} = \colvec{114}{66}, \end{equation*}
    so if \(\vect{x}_0 = \colvec{100}{80}\) is the vector encoding the initial states (the amount of customers in the current month), then
    \begin{equation*} \vect{x}_1 = P\vect{x}_0, \qquad \text{where } P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.9 \amp 0.3 \\ 0.1 \amp 0.7 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    is the vector encoding the states after 1 month (the next month).
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The important thing about this model is that what happens in the next month only depends on the states in the current month. This abstracts into the idea of a Markov chain.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 15.2.3.

    A Markov chain is a process that changes over time. It consists of:
    • A finite number of states. At each step, the process can be in any one of the states.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • At the next step, the process can either stay in the same state, or switch to a different state.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • The probability of moving from state \(j\) to state \(i\) is constant. At any step, this probability only depends on the two states \(j\) and \(i\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Remark 15.2.4.

    This is sometimes called a discrete time Markov chain, because the time steps (the intervals at which the process changes) are not continuous.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Notation and terminology:
    • We will use \(1, 2, \dots, n\) to label the states.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • We write \(\vect{x}_k\) for the vector whose \(i\)th entry is the number of members (people/objects etc.) in state \(i\) after \(k\) steps. We call \(\vect{x}_k\) a state vector. So \(\vect{x}_0\) is the initial data, called the initial state vector, and \(\vect{x}_1\) is the data after 1 step, \(\vect{x}_2\) is the data after 2 steps, and so on.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • We write \(p_{ij}\) for the probability of moving from state \(j\) to state \(i\) in 1 step, called a transition probability.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • The transition matrix is \(P = [p_{ij}]\text{,}\) where \(p_{ij}\) are the transition probabilities
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Remark 15.2.5.

    Warning: do not confuse \(p_{ij}\) with the probability of moving from state \(i\) to state \(j\text{,}\) which is \(p_{ji}\text{.}\) We write it this way to make the formulas neater later.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    For all \(i\) and \(j\text{,}\) we have \(0 \le p_{ij} \le 1\text{,}\) since they are probabilities. And
    \begin{equation*} p_{11} + p_{12} + \dots + p_{1n} = 1, \end{equation*}
    because this is the probability of moving to 1 from 1, or to 1 from 2, \(\dots\text{,}\) or to 1 from \(n\text{,}\) and state 1 must move to one of the \(n\) states. Therefore the first column of \(P\) sums to 1. Similarly, each of the other columns sum to 1, so the transition matrix \(P\) is stochastic. (Check this in ExampleÂ 15.2.1.)
    ðŸ”—

    Example 15.2.6.

    In ExampleÂ 15.2.1, let state 1 be \(W\) and state 2 be \(C\text{.}\) Then the initial state vector is
    \begin{equation*} \vect{x}_0 = \colvec{100}{80} \end{equation*}
    and the transition matrix is
    \begin{equation*} P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.9 \amp 0.3 \\ 0.1 \amp 0.7 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    After \(1\) month, \(\vect{x}_1 = P\vect{x}_0\text{.}\) After \(2\) months, \(\vect{x}_2 = P\vect{x}_1 = P(P\vect{x}_0) = P^2\vect{x}_0\text{.}\) So after \(k\) months,
    \begin{equation*} \vect{x}_k = P\vect{x}_{k-1} = \cdots = P^k\vect{x}_0. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Fact: if \(P\) is the transition matrix of a Markov chain, then the probability of moving from state \(j\) to state \(i\) in \(k\) steps is the \((i, j)\)-entry of \(P^k\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 15.2.7.

    In ExampleÂ 15.2.1, what is the probability that someone who shops at \(W\) this month will shop at \(C\) in two months time?
    ðŸ”—
    This is the probability of moving from state 1 to state 2 in 2 steps, which is the \((2, 1)\)-entry of \(P^2\text{.}\) We have
    \begin{equation*} P^2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.9 \amp 0.3 \\ 0.1 \amp 0.7 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0.9 \amp 0.3 \\ 0.1 \amp 0.7 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.84 \amp 0.48 \\ 0.16 \amp 0.52 \end{bmatrix} , \end{equation*}
    which has \((2, 1)\)-entry 0.16. Therefore the probability is 0.16.
