Example 14.3.1.

Is
\begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp 2 \amp 1 \\ -2 \amp 4 \amp 1\\ -2 \amp 2 \amp 3 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
diagonalisable? If so, write out \(A = PDP\inv\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
First, we compute the characteristic polynomial. To simplify the calculation, we will start with one step of row-reduction:
\begin{align*} \det(A-\lambda I) \amp = \begin{vmatrix} -\lambda \amp 2 \amp 1 \\ -2 \amp 4-\lambda \amp 1\\ -2 \amp 2 \amp 3-\lambda \end{vmatrix} \\ \amp = \begin{vmatrix} -\lambda \amp 2 \amp 1 \\ -2 \amp 4-\lambda \amp 1\\0 \amp \lambda -2 \amp 2-\lambda \end{vmatrix} \\ \amp = -\lambda \begin{vmatrix} 4-\lambda \amp 1 \\ \lambda - 2 \amp 2-\lambda \end{vmatrix} + 2 \begin{vmatrix} 2 \amp 1 \\ \lambda -2 \amp 2-\lambda \end{vmatrix} \\ \amp = -\lambda((4-\lambda)(2-\lambda)-(\lambda-2)) + 2(2(2-\lambda) - (\lambda - 2)) \\ \amp = -\lambda(\lambda-2)(\lambda -4-1) + 2(\lambda - 2)(-2-1) \\ \amp = - \lambda(\lambda - 2)(\lambda - 5) - 6(\lambda - 2) \\ \amp =-(\lambda-2)(\lambda(\lambda-5) + 6) \\ \amp = -(\lambda-2)(\lambda^2 - 5\lambda + 6) \\ \amp = - (\lambda-2)^2(\lambda-3)\text{.} \end{align*}
So the eigenvalues are \(\lambda = 2\) (with algebraic multiplicity 2) and \(\lambda = 3\) (with algebraic multiplicity 1). Next we calculate eigenspaces.
ðŸ”—
For \(\lambda =2\text{,}\) we have
\begin{equation*} \begin{amatrix}{c} A - 2\lambda I \amp \vect{0} \end{amatrix} = \begin{amatrix}{ccc} -2 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp 0\\ -2 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp 0\\ -2 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp 0\\ \end{amatrix} \to \begin{amatrix}{ccc} -2 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0\\ \end{amatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Since the second and third rows donâ€™t have a leading entry, we need to let \(y = s\) and \(z = t\text{,}\) for \(s, t \in \R\text{.}\) Then \(-2x + 2y + z = 0\) implies that \(x = s + \frac{1}{2}t\text{.}\) So the eigenspace is
\begin{equation*} E_2(A) = \left\{ \begin{bmatrix} s + \frac{1}{2}t \\ s\\ t \end{bmatrix} \; \middle| \; s, t \in \R\right\} = \left\{s \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1\\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + t \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{2} \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \; \middle| \; s, t \in \R\right\}\text{.} \end{equation*}
This has 2 parameters, so the geometric multiplicity of \(\lambda = 2\) is 2, which is equal to its algebraic multiplicity. Taking \(s = 1\) and \(t = 0\) gives that \([1, 1, 0]\) is an eigenvector, and taking \(s = 0\) and \(t = 1\) gives that \([\frac{1}{2}, 0, 1]\) is also an eigenvector (corresponding to a different parameter).
ðŸ”—
For \(\lambda = 3\text{,}\) we have
\begin{align*} \begin{amatrix}{c} A - 3\lambda I \amp \vect{0} \end{amatrix} = \begin{amatrix}{ccc} -3 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp 0\\ -2 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp 0\\ -2 \amp 2 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{amatrix} \amp\xrightarrow[R_3 \mapsto R_3 - \frac{2}{3}R_1]{R_2 \mapsto R_2 - \frac{2}{3}R_1} \begin{amatrix}{ccc} -3 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp -\frac{1}{3} \amp \frac{1}{3} \amp 0\\ 0 \amp \frac{2}{3} \amp -\frac{2}{3} \amp 0 \end{amatrix} \\ \amp\xrightarrow{R_3 \mapsto R_3 + 2R_2} \begin{amatrix}{ccc} -3 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp -\frac{1}{3} \amp \frac{1}{3} \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{amatrix}\text{.} \end{align*}
Let \(z = t\text{,}\) for \(t \in \R\text{.}\) Then the second row gives \(y = t\text{,}\) and the first gives \(y = t\text{.}\) Therefore the 3-eigenspace is
\begin{equation*} E_3(A) = \left\{ \begin{bmatrix} t \\ t\\ t \end{bmatrix} \;\middle|\; t \in \R\right\}\text{.} \end{equation*}
This has 1 parameter, so the geometric multiplicity of \(\lambda = 3\) is 1, which is equal to its algebraic multiplicity. Taking \(t = 1\text{,}\) an eigenvector is \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \text{.}\) Therefore both eigenvalues have geometric multiplicity = algebraic multiplicity, so \(A\) is diagonalisable, and we can write \(A = PDP\inv\) as
\begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp \frac{1}{2} \\ 1 \amp 1 \amp 0\\ 1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 2 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp \frac{1}{2} \\ 1 \amp 1 \amp 0\\ 1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \inv\text{.} \end{equation*}
The columns of \(P\) are the eigenvectors of \(A\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
Notice that since \(\lambda = 2\) has algebraic multiplicity 2, we needed to find 2 eigenvectors corresponding to 2 different parameters in \(E_2(A)\text{,}\) and the eigenvalue 2 appears twice in the diagonal matrix \(D\) (in the corresponding positions to its eigenvectors in \(P\)).
