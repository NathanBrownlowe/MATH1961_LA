Theorem 7.2.1.

A \(2\times 2\) matrix
\begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} a \amp b \\ c\amp d \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
is invertible if and only if \(ad-bc \neq 0\text{.}\) In this case, we have
\begin{equation*} A\inv = \frac{1}{ad-bc} \begin{bmatrix} d \amp -b \\ -c \amp a \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

ðŸ”—

Proof.

Suppose \(ad-bc\ne 0\text{.}\) Then
\begin{align*} A = \begin{bmatrix} a \amp b \\ c\amp d \end{bmatrix} \left(\frac{1}{ad-bc} \begin{bmatrix} d \amp -b \\ -c \amp a \end{bmatrix} \right) \amp = \frac{1}{ad-bc} \begin{bmatrix} a \amp b \\ c\amp d \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} d \amp -b \\ -c \amp a \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = \frac{1}{ad-bc} \begin{bmatrix} ad - bc \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp -cb+ da \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} = I_2\text{.} \end{align*}
Similarly one can show that
\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{ad-bc} \begin{bmatrix} d \amp -b \\ -c \amp a \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a \amp b \\ c\amp d \end{bmatrix} = I_2\text{.} \end{equation*}
This shows that \(A\) is invertible, with the desired inverse.
ðŸ”—
Conversely, suppose that \(ad- bc = 0\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
Case 1: If \(a = 0\text{,}\) then \(bc = 0\text{,}\) so \(b = 0\) or \(c = 0\text{.}\) If \(b = 0\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp 0 \\ c \amp d \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a' \amp b' \\ c' \amp d' \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp 0 \\ a'c + c'd \amp b'c + dd' \end{bmatrix} \ne I_2 \end{equation*}
since the \((1, 1)\)-entry is zero instead of one. If \(c = 0\text{,}\) then
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} a' \amp b' \\ c' \amp d' \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp b \\ 0 \amp d \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp a'b + b'd \\ 0 \amp bc' + dd' \end{bmatrix} \ne I_2\text{.} \end{equation*}
Since \(a', b', c', d' \in \R\) were arbitrary, this means that \(A\) has no inverse.
