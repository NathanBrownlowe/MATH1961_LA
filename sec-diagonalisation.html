    Section 13.2 Diagonalisation

    Definition 13.2.1.

    An \(n\times n\) matrix \(A\) is diagonalisable if there is an invertible matrix \(P\) and a diagonal matrix \(D\) such that
    \begin{equation*} A = PDP\inv\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 13.2.2.

    If a matrix \(D\) is already diagonal, intuitively it should be diagonalisable. This is true; using the definition, we can take \(P = I\text{.}\) Then \(D = IDI\inv\) shows that \(D\) is diagonalisable.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Let \(A = PDP\inv\) be an \(n\times n\) diagonalisable matrix (where \(D\) is diagonal). Let
    \begin{equation*} P = \begin{bmatrix} \vect{x}_1 \amp \vect{x}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \vect{x}_n \end{bmatrix} , \qquad D = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 \amp \amp \amp \\ \amp \lambda_2 \amp \amp \\ \amp \amp \ddots \amp \\ \amp \amp \amp \lambda_n \end{bmatrix} , \end{equation*}
    where the blank elements of \(D\) are zero, and the columns of \(P\) are the vectors \(\vect{x}_i\text{.}\) Then \(A = PDP\inv\) implies that \(AP = PD\text{.}\) Writing this out gives
    \begin{equation*} A \begin{bmatrix} \vect{x}_1 \amp \vect{x}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \vect{x}_n \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \vect{x}_1 \amp \vect{x}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \vect{x}_n \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 \amp \amp \amp \\ \amp \lambda_2 \amp \amp \\ \amp \amp \ddots \amp \\ \amp \amp \amp \lambda_n \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The left-hand side equals \([A\vect{x}_1, A\vect{x}_2,\dots, A\vect{x}_n]\text{,}\) which you are asked to calculate in NEED REF below. The right-hand side equals \([\lambda_1\vect{x}_1, \lambda_2\vect{x}_2, \dots, \lambda_n\vect{x}_n]\text{.}\) Comparing columns, this shows that \(A\vect{x}_i = \lambda\vect{x}_i\) for all \(i=1, \dots, n\text{.}\) Moreover, each column \(\vect{x}_i\) is nonzero since \(P\) is invertible. Therefore the eigenvectors of \(A\) are \(\vect{x}_i\) (which are the columns of \(P\)) and the corresponding eigenvalues are \(\lambda_i\) (which are the corresponding diagonal entries of \(D\)).
    ðŸ”—
    Similarly, if \(A\) has \(n\) distinct eigenvalues \(\vect{x}_1,\dots, \vect{x}_n\) such that \(\begin{bmatrix} \vect{x}_1 \amp \vect{x}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \vect{x}_n \end{bmatrix}\) is invertible, then \(A\) is diagonalisable and \(A = PDP\inv\text{,}\) where \(D\) is the diagonal matrix of eigenvalues, as above. (To see this, \(P\) consisting of eigenvalues means \(AP = \begin{bmatrix} A\vect{x}_1 \amp A\vect{x}_2 \amp \cdots \amp A\vect{x}_n \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1\vect{x}_1 \amp \lambda_2\vect{x}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \lambda_n\vect{x}_n \end{bmatrix} = PD.\) Multiplying by \(P\inv\text{,}\) we conclude \(A = PDP\inv\text{.}\))
    ðŸ”—
    We summarise the previous calculations in the following theorem.
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 13.2.3.

    An \(n\times n\) matrix \(A\) is diagonalisable if and only if there are \(n\) eigenvectors \(\vect{x}_1,\dots, \vect{x}_n\) such that \(\begin{bmatrix} \vect{x}_1 \amp \vect{x}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \vect{x}_n \end{bmatrix}\) is invertible. In this case, \(A = PDP\inv\) for
    \begin{equation*} P = \begin{bmatrix} \vect{x}_1 \amp \vect{x}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \vect{x}_n \end{bmatrix} , \qquad D = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 \amp \amp \amp \\ \amp \lambda_2 \amp \amp \\ \amp \amp \ddots \amp \\ \amp \amp \amp \lambda_n \end{bmatrix} , \end{equation*}
    where each \(\lambda_i\) is the eigenvalue for \(\vect{x}_i\text{,}\) and the blank elements of \(D\) are zero.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    For this theorem to be useful, we need a convenient criterion for when \(\begin{bmatrix} \vect{x}_1 \amp \vect{x}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \vect{x}_n \end{bmatrix}\) is invertible. (We know that this is when its determinant is nonzero, but we would still need a way to check that.) In fact, it is invertible whenever our two types of multiplicity are equal.
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 13.2.4.

    Let \(A\) be a \(n\times n\) matrix. There is an invertible matrix \(\begin{bmatrix} \vect{x}_1 \amp \vect{x}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \vect{x}_n \end{bmatrix}\) consisting of eigenvectors of \(A\) if and only if the algebraic multiplicity of every eigenvalue equals its geometric multiplicity.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Combining TheoremÂ 13.2.3 and TheoremÂ 13.2.4 gives the following criterion, which is how we will check in practice whether a matrix is diagonalisable.
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 13.2.5.

    Let \(A\) be an \(n\times n\) matrix. Then \(A\) is diagonalisable if and only if the algebraic multiplicity of every eigenvalue equals its geometric multiplicity. In this case, \(A = PDP\inv\) where \(P\) and \(D\) are the same as in TheoremÂ 13.2.3.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    So in an example, we can calculate the eigenspaces and then check the multiplicities to see if they match. An important special case is when all the eigenvalues are different.
    ðŸ”—

    Corollary 13.2.6.

    If \(A\) is a \(n\times n\) matrix with \(n\) distinct eigenvalues, then \(A\) is diagonalisable.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    This is because by RemarkÂ 12.3.5, if \(A\) has distinct eigenvalues then every eigenvalue has geometric and algebraic multiplicity equal to 1. So the multiplicities match. Then by TheoremÂ 13.2.5, \(A\) is diagonalisable.
