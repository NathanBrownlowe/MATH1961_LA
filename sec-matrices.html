    Example 6.1.2.

    Consider the matrices
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \end{bmatrix} , \qquad \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 4\end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The first is a row matrix and the second is a column matrix.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Notice how these special matrices look essentially the same as vectors.
    ðŸ”—
    If \(A\) is an \(m\times n\) matrix, we can write
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \amp \cdots \amp a_{1n} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \amp \cdots \amp a_{2n} \\ \vdots \amp \vdots \amp \ddots \amp \vdots \\ a_{m1} \amp a_{m2} \amp \cdots \amp a_{mn} \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    We may also write
    \begin{equation*} A = [a_{ij}] \qquad \text{or} \qquad A = [a_{ij}]_{m\times n} \end{equation*}
    for short. If the columns of \(A\) are the column vectors \(\vect{a}_1\text{,}\) \(\vect{a}_2, \ldots,\vect{a}_n\text{,}\) we may write
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} \vect{a}_1 \amp \vect{a}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \vect{a}_n \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    If the rows of \(B\) are the row vectors \(\vect{b}_1\text{,}\) \(\vect{b}_2, \ldots, \vect{b}_m\text{,}\) we may write
    \begin{equation*} B = \begin{bmatrix} \vect{b}_1 \\ \vect{b}_2 \\ \vdots \\ \vect{b}_m \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Find the size and the \((2, 1)-\)entry of the following matrices.

    Definition 6.1.5.

    Two \(m \times n\) matrices \(A\) and \(B\) are equal if their corresponding entries are equal.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    In other words, \(A = B\) if and only if \(a_{ij} = b_{ij}\) for all \(i = 1, \ldots, n\text{,}\) \(j= 1, \ldots, n\text{.}\)

    Example 6.1.6.

    Let \(A = [a_{ij}]_{2\times 2}\) where \(a_{ij}=2^{i-1}3^{j-1}\) and \(B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 3 \\ 2 \amp 6 \end{bmatrix} \text{.}\) Then \(A=B\) because
