    Section 10.2 The rank-nullity theorem

    This section will culminate in a fundamental result called the rank-nullity theorem, but before we get to this theorem, we will consider the problem of finding bases for the row, column and null spaces of a given matrix. We will start with the general, fool-proof method for finding these bases. We will then illustrate this with an example, before giving a justification (rather than a careful proof) of why these methods work.
    ðŸ”—
    Let \(A\) be a matrix, and let \(R\) be its RREF. To find a basis for:
    ðŸ”—
    1. For \(\operatorname{row}(A)\text{:}\) Take the nonzero row vectors of \(R\) for a basis of \(\operatorname{row}(A)\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. For \(\operatorname{col}(A)\text{:}\) Take the column vectors of \(A\) in the same positions as the columns of \(R\) that contain a leading \(1\) for a basis of \(\operatorname{col}(A)\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    3. For \(\operatorname{null}(A)\text{:}\) Solve \(R\vect{x}=\vect{0}\) and write the subspace of solutions as a span by separating the parameters. The vectors in the span will be a basis for \(\operatorname{null}(A)\text{.}\)

      ðŸ”—

    Example 10.2.1.

    Consider \(A\) and its RREF \(R\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} A=\begin{bmatrix}1\amp 1\amp 3\ amp 1\ amp 6\\ 2\amp -1\amp 0\amp 1\amp -1\\ -3\amp 2\amp 1\amp -2\amp 1\\ 4\amp 1\amp 6\amp 1\amp 3\end{bmatrix},\, R=\begin{bmatrix}1\amp 0\amp 1\ amp 0\ amp -1\\ 0\amp 1\amp 2\amp 0\amp 3\\ 0\amp 0\amp 0\amp 1\amp 4\\ 0\amp 0\amp 0\amp 0\amp 0\end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Find bases for \(\operatorname{row}(A)\text{,}\) \(\operatorname{col}(A)\text{,}\) and \(\operatorname{null}(A)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Following the rules above, we have
    1. For \(\operatorname{row}(A)\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} \left\{\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\\1\\0\\-1\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}0\\1\\2\\0\\3\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}0\\0\\0\\1\\4\end{bmatrix},\right\} \text{ is a basis for }\operatorname{row}(A). \end{equation*}
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. For \(\operatorname{col}(A)\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} \left\{\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\\-3\\4\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1\\1\\2\\1\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1\\1\\-2\\1\end{bmatrix},\right\} \text{ is a basis for }\operatorname{col}(A). \end{equation*}
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    3. For \(\operatorname{null}(A)\text{:}\) Let \(x_3=s,\, x_5=t,\, s,t\in\R\text{.}\) Then
      \begin{equation*} x_4+5x_5=0\implies x_4=-5t, \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} x_2+2x_3+3x_5=0\implies x_2=-2s-3t, \end{equation*}
      and
      \begin{equation*} x_1+x_3-x_5=0\implies x_1=-s+t. \end{equation*}
      So
      \begin{align*} \operatorname{null}(A) \amp =\left\{\begin{bmatrix}-s+t\\-2s-3t\\s\\-5t\\t\end{bmatrix} : s,t\in\R\right\} \\ \amp =\left\{s\begin{bmatrix}-1\\-2\\1\\0\\0\end{bmatrix} + t\begin{bmatrix}1\\-3\\0\\-5\\1\end{bmatrix} : s,t\in\R\right\} \\ \amp =\lspan\left(\begin{bmatrix}-1\\-2\\1\\0\\0\end{bmatrix},\begin{bmatrix}1\\-3\\0\\-5\\1\end{bmatrix}\right). \end{align*}
      So \(\left\{\begin{bmatrix}-1\\-2\\1\\0\\0\end{bmatrix},\begin{bmatrix}1\\-3\\0\\-5\\1\end{bmatrix}\right\}\) is a basis for \(\operatorname{null}(A)\text{.}\)

    Why does the row space method work?
    • We have \(\operatorname{row}(A)=\operatorname{row}(R)\) because we get from \(A\) to \(R\text{.}\) So any basis for \(\operatorname{row}(R)\) is a basis for \(\operatorname{row}(A)\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • The nonzero rows of \(R\) are linearly independent as the leading 1s are in different positions (with 0s in the other corresponding positions).
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Why does the column space method work?
    ðŸ”—
    Unlike for the row spaces, in general we have \(\operatorname{col}(A)=\operatorname{col}(R)\text{,}\) but we do have \(\operatorname{dim}(\operatorname{col}(A))=\operatorname{dim}(\operatorname{col}(R))\text{.}\) To see why, let \(\vect{c}_1,\dots,\vect{c}_n\) be the columns of \(A\text{,}\) and let let \(\vect{r}_1,\dots,\vect{r}_n\) be the columns of \(R\text{.}\) Then we have
    \begin{align*} a_1\vect{c}_1+\cdots+a_n\vect{c}_n = \vect{0} \amp \iff A\begin{bmatrix}a_1\\\vdots\\a_n\end{bmatrix}= \vect{0}\\ \amp \iff R\begin{bmatrix}a_1\\\vdots\\a_n\end{bmatrix}= \vect{0}\\ \amp \iff a_1\vect{r}_1+\cdots+a_n\vect{r}_n = \vect{0}. \end{align*}
    What are these implications saying? This means that linearly independent vectors amongst the \(\vect{r}_1,\dots,\vect{r}_n\) correspond to linearly independent vectors amongst the \(\vect{c}_1,\dots,\vect{c}_n\text{.}\) In particular, a basis for the column space of \(R\) corresponds (in terms of vector position) to a basis for the column space of \(A\text{.}\) Finally, the columns of \(R\) with a leading 1 are linearly independent because they are standard basis vectors, and they span the column space of \(R\text{;}\) hence they are a basis. By the previous discussion, the vectors in the same positions of the columns of \(A\) are a basis for \(\operatorname{col}(A)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    We can now present the rank-nullity theorem. We start with the definitions.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 10.2.2.

    Let \(A\) be a matrix. Then the rank of \(A\text{,}\) denoted \(\operatorname{rank}(A)\text{,}\) is defined to be \(\operatorname{rank}(A):=\operatorname{dim}(\operatorname{col}(A))\text{.}\) The nullity of \(A\text{,}\) denoted \(\operatorname{nullity}(A)\text{,}\) is defined to be \(\operatorname{nullity}(A):=\operatorname{dim}(\operatorname{null}(A))\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Remark 10.2.3.

    Note that it follows from the discussion above that the rank of a matrix is the number of leading 1s in its RREF, and the nullity of a matrix is the number of parameters appearing in the solution space; that is, the number of columns of the RREF without a leading 1.

    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 10.2.4.

    If \(A\) is \(m\times n\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \operatorname{rank}(A)+\operatorname{nullity}(A)=n. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    For \(R\) the RREF of \(A\) we have
    \begin{align*} n \amp = \#\text{ columns of }R \\ \amp = (\#\text{ cols of }R\text{ with a leading 1})+ (\#\text{ cols of }R\text{ without a leading 1}) \\ \amp = \operatorname{rank}(A)+\operatorname{nullity}(A). \end{align*}
